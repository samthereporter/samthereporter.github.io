The Empty Desk Epidemic: Tracing Chronic Absenteeism in Nebraska

Before the COVID-19 pandemic, chronic absenteeism in Nebraska hovered around 10 percent. It was a known challenge, but a manageable one.

Then came the disruptions.

During the 2020-2021 academic year, the rate of students missing significant classroom time accelerated to nearly 24 percent. But while classrooms have long since returned to normal operations, student attendance has not. During the 2023-2024 academic year, the chronic absentee rate remained stubbornly high at 21.18 percent.

Missing 10% of the academic year equates to 17.5 lost learning days. Over time, these empty desks translate to lower reading and numeracy skills, reduced graduation rates, and a widening gap in postsecondary enrollment.

So, who is missing from Nebraska’s classrooms, and what happens when they don't return?

A Statewide Challenge, Not Just a City Problem

It is a common misconception that chronic absenteeism is primarily an urban issue. In Nebraska, the data tells a different story.

Rates of chronic absenteeism are relatively similar across all 93 counties. Urban counties with sizable districts like Lancaster and Douglas do not show noticeable differences in their percentage of chronic absentees compared to their rural neighbors.

In fact, the highest rates are found in more rural contexts. Thurston County averages a 48 percent chronic absenteeism rate, uniquely influenced by two of its districts residing on indigenous tribal lands. McPherson County closely follows with an average rate of 46 percent.

The Middle School Cliff

Attendance habits shift dramatically as students grow older.

In the 2023-2024 academic year, fourth graders exhibited the lowest amount of chronic absenteeism at 14.18 percent. However, a significant shift occurs during the transition to middle school. Sixth-grade students demonstrate a sudden five percent spike in chronic absenteeism.

This upward trajectory continues throughout high school, peaking with twelfth-grade students showing the greatest chronic absenteeism rate at 36.98 percent.

When these students miss school, it isn't just for a day or two. While typically-attending students miss an average of 6.33 to 7.53 days a year, chronic absentees miss an average of 20.52 to 35.62 school days.

Why Are They Missing?

The reasons behind the absences also reveal a stark divide.

Students who maintain regular attendance primarily miss school for excused reasons (30.3 percent) or illness and medical leave (45.0 percent).

Chronically absent students, however, are far more likely to miss school due to unexcused or behavioral reasons. The percentage of absences due to skipping school is more than five times higher for chronic absentees (9 percent) than for their peers (1.6 percent). Furthermore, 'Unknown unexcused absences' account for nearly 40.7 percent of their missed time, nearly double the rate of typically-attending students.

The Academic Toll

The impact of losing weeks of instructional time becomes highly visible in statewide assessments.

Across all grades, chronic absentees demonstrate lower rates of proficiency in science, math, and English language arts. This achievement gap widens significantly as students get older.

By the eleventh grade, the disparity is glaring. Only 20.1 percent of chronically absent eleventh-graders are proficient in math, compared to 50.5 percent of their regularly-attending peers. Similar gaps exist in English language arts (24.0 percent vs. 52.9 percent) and science (26.8 percent vs. 57.0 percent).

This trend mirrors ACT performance, where typically-attending eleventh graders score an average of 3.49 points higher on their composite scores.

Graduation and Beyond

Ultimately, chronic absenteeism fractures the pathway to postsecondary success and economic self-sufficiency.

Between 2021 and 2024, the odds of graduating on-time were nearly six times greater for twelfth-graders who were not chronically absent. While 95.8 percent of typically-attending seniors graduated within four years, only 79.5 percent of chronically absent seniors did the same.

The gap widens further after graduation. 70.9 percent of regular attenders enroll in a postsecondary institution. For chronic absentees, that number drops to 48.7 percent.

Reversing the Trend

The historic surge in Nebraska’s chronic absenteeism has not naturally self-corrected.

Because the 'typical' absent student encompasses a wide demographic—including higher rates among Indigenous students (47.1 percent), Black students (39.6 percent), and students participating in Special Education (23.7 percent) or English Language programs (26.8 percent)—blanket solutions are rarely effective.

The Nebraska Department of Education has initiated a legislative priority focused on cutting chronic absenteeism in half. By moving away from punitive responses and instead investing in early interventions, removing access barriers, and fostering community buy-in, Nebraska aims to bring its students back to the classroom.
